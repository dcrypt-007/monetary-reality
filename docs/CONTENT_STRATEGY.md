# Content Strategy — Monetary Reality

## How the Argument Should Be Framed

The site's argument follows a logical chain:

1. **Observation**: Prices across the economy have been persistently rising for decades
2. **Question**: What causes broad, persistent price increases?
3. **Mechanism**: Sustained price increases across an entire economy require the money supply to grow faster than real output
4. **Evidence**: This pattern is observable in U.S. data, international data, and historical cases going back millennia
5. **Implication**: The monetary system — deficit spending, central bank accommodation, credit expansion — is the structural driver of long-term purchasing power erosion
6. **Nuance**: Individual price changes have many causes, but the system-wide trend is monetary

This is not a conspiracy theory. It is mainstream monetary economics — just rarely explained clearly to the public.

## Tone Guidelines

### DO:
- Present data first, conclusions second
- Use plain language wherever possible
- Acknowledge complexity and counterarguments
- Cite specific data sources
- Use phrases like "the data suggests", "historically", "the pattern shows"
- Let the reader draw their own conclusions from well-presented evidence

### DON'T:
- Use alarmist language ("collapse is imminent", "the system is broken")
- Make predictions or forecasts
- Attack specific politicians, parties, or institutions by name in an emotional way
- Use conspiratorial framing ("they don't want you to know")
- Moralize or preach
- Assume the reader agrees with you

## How to Avoid Ideological Drift

This is critical. The site will attract audiences from various ideological perspectives. To maintain credibility:

1. **Never align with a political party** — The monetary dynamics described transcend party lines. Both major parties have contributed to deficit spending and monetary expansion.

2. **Never become a gold/crypto advocacy site** — The site is about understanding the current system, not selling alternatives.

3. **Acknowledge what monetary expansion enables** — Deficit spending funds wars, but also social programs, infrastructure, and crisis response. The site should explain mechanisms, not moralize about whether spending is "good" or "bad."

4. **Present counterarguments fairly** — Modern Monetary Theory, Keynesian stimulus arguments, and other perspectives should be represented honestly, even if the site ultimately argues against them.

5. **Maintain intellectual humility** — Economics is complex. The site should be confident in its core thesis while acknowledging the limits of any single framework.

## How Bitcoin Should Be Treated

Bitcoin and other cryptocurrencies may be discussed in the following limited contexts:

- As one data point in comparisons of asset performance during monetary expansion
- As a historical response to monetary instability (alongside gold, real estate, etc.)
- In the context of monetary collapse case studies where citizens sought alternatives

Bitcoin should NEVER:
- Be presented as a solution or recommendation
- Appear in the site's branding, hero section, or primary messaging
- Be the focal point of any page
- Be promoted, shilled, or advocated for

The site is about the diagnosis (monetary expansion drives price increases), not about any particular prescription.

## How Case Studies Should Be Structured

Every historical case study should follow this framework:

### 1. Context & Pre-conditions
- What was the monetary and fiscal situation before the crisis?
- What political/economic pressures existed?

### 2. Trigger Events
- What specific events accelerated the crisis?
- Was there a tipping point?

### 3. Monetary Mechanisms
- How was the money supply expanded? (Printing, credit, deficit spending)
- Through what institutional channels?

### 4. Price Response
- How did prices respond? Over what timeframe?
- What data is available?

### 5. Social & Economic Consequences
- Who was most affected?
- How did daily life change?
- What were the wealth distribution effects?

### 6. Resolution
- How did the crisis end?
- What replaced the failed monetary arrangement?

### 7. Lessons & Parallels
- What general principles does this case illustrate?
- What parallels exist to modern situations?
- What are the limits of the comparison?

## Content Types

### Explainers
Short, focused pieces that explain one concept clearly. Examples: "What is M2?", "How do banks create money?", "What is the Cantillon Effect?"

### Case Studies
Historical analyses following the framework above. Rich with data and primary sources.

### Deep Dives
Extended analyses of specific phenomena. Longer-form, more technical, heavily sourced.

### Tool Documentation
Each interactive tool should have an accompanying explainer covering the underlying model, assumptions, and data sources.
