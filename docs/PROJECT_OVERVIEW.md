# Project Overview — Monetary Reality

## Mission

Monetary Reality is a public educational website that makes the mechanics of monetary systems visible, understandable, and verifiable. The site demonstrates — through data, historical evidence, and interactive tools — that persistent, economy-wide price increases are primarily driven by monetary expansion, deficit spending, central banking, and credit expansion.

## Target Audience

- Data-curious individuals who notice rising costs but don't understand why
- Students and young adults exploring economics for the first time
- Skeptics who want evidence, not ideology
- Educators looking for clear, sourced explanations of monetary phenomena
- Anyone tired of being told "inflation is transitory" without context

## Site Tone

The tone must be:

- **Forensic** — presenting evidence methodically
- **Credible** — every claim sourced, counterarguments acknowledged
- **Calm** — no hysteria, no doom rhetoric, no alarmism
- **Data-driven** — charts and data over opinions
- **Accessible** — a curious 18-year-old should be able to follow along

The site should feel like a research lab or data observatory, not a political campaign or a crypto landing page.

## Strategic Guardrails

### This site IS:
- A public educational resource
- Data-driven and source-transparent
- Focused on monetary mechanics and purchasing power
- Nuanced — acknowledging complexity while maintaining its core thesis

### This site IS NOT:
- A Bitcoin or cryptocurrency promotion site
- A conspiracy theory platform
- A partisan political operation
- Financial advice
- A doom-and-gloom outlet

### Bitcoin Policy
Bitcoin may appear in future content **only** as one comparative asset in specific monetary-collapse visualizations — never as the site's identity, recommendation, or conclusion. The site is about understanding the monetary system, not about promoting any particular alternative.

### Nuance Requirements
The site must acknowledge:
- Supply shocks exist and affect prices
- Regulation and policy affect specific sectors
- Not every price increase has a monetary cause
- The thesis is about long-term, system-wide, persistent effects
- Correlation and causation must be carefully distinguished
